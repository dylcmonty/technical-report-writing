# 5. Silicon co-systems, learned perception, neural decoding, and the limits of physical information

## Scope and conceptual map

Your notes combine four separable questions that are worth keeping distinct if you want a science-based article or fictional setting to feel coherent. First, there is a chemistry question: whether silicon can supply a family of stable, modular architectures analogous to carbon nanostructures or even to organic biochemistry. Second, there is a neurodevelopment question: why sensory organs alone do not produce perception unless the brain has learned how to interpret their signals. Third, there is a decoding question: how much of a physical information system can be inferred from inputs, outputs, and internal state measurements. A crucial terminological correction is that **silicon** is the element, whereas **silicones** are usually polysiloxane polymers built around repeating Si–O–Si backbones with organic side groups. In current science, those two families behave very differently in biology. Silicon allotropes and semiconductors matter for electronics and nanostructures; silicones matter for inert, flexible biomaterials and medical devices. ## Silicon and silicone as candidates for a parallel bodily substrate

At the level of basic chemistry, carbon remains overwhelmingly favored over silicon for water-based life. The strongest reason is not that silicon is “uninteresting,” but that in oxygen-rich, aqueous environments it is driven toward silicates and silica, which sharply reduces the accessible diversity of soluble, information-rich, metabolically useful compounds. The most comprehensive review on silicon as a possible basis for life concludes that silicon’s chemical capacity in water is highly limited relative to carbon, even though organosilicon compounds are real and useful. citeturn6search0turn28search20

That does not mean silicon lacks nanoarchitectures. It means their landscape differs from carbon’s. Real silicon candidates include metastable silicon allotropes, clathrate frameworks, molecular silicon clusters, and the 2D material silicene. But the comparison to graphene, fullerenes, or nanotubes is incomplete: free-standing silicene is difficult to stabilize, oxidizes readily, and remains strongly substrate-dependent, and recent work argues that a canonical silicene phase on Ag(111) is not supported. Silicon clathrates and other low-density allotropes are scientifically important because they can have unusual band structures and optoelectronic behavior, but they are not a one-to-one biological analogue of carbon nanoallotropes. citeturn32search2turn32search4turn32search10turn32search21turn32search17

If the goal is **modular silicon-based building blocks** compatible with biology, the closest real families are not pure silicon allotropes but **organosilicon hybrids**. Polyhedral oligomeric silsesquioxanes (POSS) provide cage-like Si–O frameworks with tunable organic functionalization. Silicones and other polysiloxanes provide flexible, chemically stable biomaterials. Porous silicon offers tunable biodegradability, large surface area, and optical/electronic utility. Silicon carbide offers a wide-bandgap, chemically inert, biocompatible semiconductor platform for durable biointerfaces. Those families are not “silicon organisms,” but they are exactly the kinds of modular, repeatable, property-rich units that can plausibly support a secondary technological subsystem inside a body. citeturn27search10turn27search2turn27search19turn27search11turn36search7turn36search1

That said, coexistence with the body is not equivalent to noninterference. Silicones are widely used because of flexibility, stability, and relative inertness, yet implanted biomaterials still trigger inflammation, foreign-body responses, and fibrotic encapsulation. Silicon-compatible medical materials are therefore “cooperative” rather than invisible. This matters for your concept: a dual substrate in one body would need immunological management, compartmentalization, and surface engineering rather than mere elemental substitution. citeturn33search1turn33search3turn33search7turn35search3

That result does **not** imply silicon-based life. It does show that carbon life can be taught to host selective organosilicon chemistry—exactly the kind of partial interoperability your framework wants. citeturn28search4turn28search19turn28search2

## Why a transplanted eye is not enough for sight

The visual example in your notes is substantially correct, but it needs a precise formulation. In humans, a whole eye can now be transplanted as living tissue, but restored vision has not been achieved. In the 2023 landmark procedure at entity["organization","NYU Langone Health","new york, ny, us"], the transplanted eye remained viable more than a year later, with preserved blood flow, normal pressure, and evidence that rods and cones survived, but the patient did not regain sight. Both institutional and review sources identify the unresolved problem as functional reconnection of the optic nerve and its downstream integration with the brain. citeturn37view0turn8view4turn7search4

That clinical limitation lines up with developmental neuroscience. The long-running entity["organization","Project Prakash","vision research india"] studies of children who gained sight late after congenital blindness show that vision is not simply “turned on” when retinal input arrives. Newly sighted participants initially fail tasks like immediate seen-versus-felt object matching, then improve over days and weeks, indicating rapid learning rather than innate, fully formed visual interpretation. A 2012 review of these cases concluded that some visual functions remain plastic late into development, but early visual experience remains crucial for normal acquisition of key abilities. citeturn9search3turn10view0turn9search4

The deeper finding is that different visual functions depend differently on early experience. More recent longitudinal work on congenital cataract reversal shows robust gains in object identification, but much more persistent impairments in face identification and configural processing after prolonged early deprivation. In other words, the brain can learn a great deal after delayed sight onset, but it does not acquire every visual competence equally well or automatically. citeturn10view2turn7search7

## What neural decoding can genuinely recover

The cat-vision example refers to a real and important lineage of experiments, but it is often overstated in popular retellings. The classic 1999 study reconstructed natural scenes from ensemble responses in the cat lateral geniculate nucleus using a linear decoder. Those reconstructions were blurry but recognizable. Later work in humans reconstructed approximate movie content from fMRI responses during natural viewing, showing that dynamic visual information can be decoded under naturalistic conditions. These studies demonstrate that structured sensory content can be inferred from neural activity; they do **not** show that researchers have extracted the animal’s or person’s full subjective experience. citeturn11search1turn11search5turn11search0

The field has advanced sharply with modern generative models. Diffusion-based pipelines can now reconstruct complex natural scenes from fMRI with much better recovery of layout and semantic content than older methods, and Bayesian deep-learning approaches have also reported successful reconstruction of both seen and imagined images from brain activity. But these systems depend on training sets, modeling assumptions, and statistical correspondences between measured activity and latent image features. They recover **stimulus-correlated structure**, not unmediated qualia. citeturn12view2turn12view3

That distinction matters for your philosophy. Neural decoding works because brains are physical systems with lawful signals and because experimenters can constrain the task, the measurement space, and the decoding objective. It does not imply that “anything physical can be decoded from enough observations” in the unrestricted sense. What it supports is the narrower claim that **many representational variables in physical information systems become decodable when the measurement channel, perturbation structure, and target variable are well defined**. citeturn14search1turn12view2turn12view3

## A better formal language for your information-system idea

The strongest version of your statement—roughly, “anything physical and reactive to inputs can be decoded by observing states across enough inputs”—is too strong as a universal claim. Formal sciences replace that intuition with more precise concepts: **observability**, which asks whether internal states can be inferred from outputs; **structural identifiability**, which asks whether model parameters can be uniquely recovered; and **state-space reconstruction**, which asks whether underlying dynamics can be reconstructed from observed time series. These properties can fail even in relatively simple systems, so universal decodability is false in general. citeturn18search0turn18search6turn17search8turn16search20

Your idea becomes much more defensible when narrowed to **physical information systems under controlled perturbation and restricted model classes**. In deterministic dynamical systems, Takens-style embeddings show that a scalar time series can, under appropriate conditions, reconstruct an attractor that preserves the structure of the underlying dynamics. In neuroscience and systems biology, perturbation-based system identification and hybrid mechanistic/data-driven models likewise show that carefully designed inputs can reveal hidden structure much more effectively than passive observation alone. That is very close to the “single-variable input, multivariable output” intuition in your notes. citeturn16search15turn17search15turn17search18turn15view2

There is also a useful correction to the phrase “information is the reality that exists but does not exist.” The best established physical view is closer to Landauer’s claim that **information is physical**: information is not a separate ghostly substance but a pattern instantiated in material states and constrained by the actual physics of storage, noise, energy, and computation. That supports your instinct that “reality is the fabric within which information systems exist,” but it does not imply a second realm adjacent to matter. It implies that information is real only through physical embodiment. citeturn14search19turn14search15

This reframing also sharpens the AI analogy. A running digital model is a physical dynamical system implemented in hardware, not a disembodied semantic field. At the same time, contemporary interpretability research increasingly treats AI as something that can be reverse-engineered into circuits, features, and causal subcomputations rather than treated as a pure black box. So your comparison works best if framed as **layered physical information systems with different substrates and timescales**, not as a mystical opposition between digital information and physical reality. citeturn31search0turn31search13turn14search19

## Quantum language, sleep, and where evidence stops

This is the place where your notes move furthest from established evidence. Quantum biology is real, but in a bounded way. Mainstream reviews discuss quantum-relevant processes in photon capture for vision, excitonic transport in photosynthesis, and radical-pair models of magnetoreception. Those are specific biochemical settings. Even within that literature, neural quantum effects are treated as the most speculative end of the field. One review explicitly notes that vision requires a quantum or semiclassical description of photon absorption but does **not** require coherence or entanglement, and it treats neural-process proposals separately as speculative. citeturn21view0

By contrast, the idea that macroscopic thought, intention, trauma, or “place memory” is carried by persistent quantum entanglement in ordinary environments is not supported by accepted evidence. Classic decoherence arguments hold that brain-scale quantum states relevant to cognition would decohere far too quickly to function like a quantum computer, and later pro-quantum-brain reviews still describe the matter as controversial rather than established. The cautious scientific position is therefore not “impossible,” but “speculative, unverified, and far from explanatory necessity.” citeturn20search11turn22search0turn22search2

The sleep claim in your notes can also be reformulated without quantum language. What has strong support is that sleep is an active state for memory consolidation, with coordinated slow oscillations, spindles, and hippocampal ripples organizing replay and hippocampo-cortical communication. Recent human work shows that spindle-locked ripples are associated with memory reactivation during NREM sleep. None of that requires electrons in neurons to enter a cognitively meaningful macroscopic superposition state. The best-supported sleep science is replay, coupling, plasticity, and network-level dynamics. citeturn21view1turn21view2turn19search18

The “eerie feeling” of places can also be described more conservatively. Environmental-psychology work shows that ambiguous threat cues, spatial uncertainty, uncanny mismatches, and affective expectations can systematically drive unease and felt presence. That does not disprove every extraordinary interpretation, but it means the burden of proof sits with the quantum-imprint hypothesis, not with ordinary perceptual and affective explanations. citeturn30search8turn30search24turn30search1

## Science-grounded extrapolations for fiction and future design

If you want a plausible future version of your idea, the most defensible architecture is **not** a second silicon organism. It is a **developmentally integrated silicon-derived co-system**: durable silicon carbide or silicon-based electronics for signal routing and sensing, silicone or polysiloxane materials for flexible encapsulation and interfaces, porous silicon or silsesquioxane structures for tunable chemistry, and selective biochemical gateways where the carbon body and the engineered subsystem exchange information. That is compatible with existing trends in neuroprosthetics, cochlear implants, sensory substitution, and implantable bioelectronics, all of which rely on plasticity rather than on perfect elemental isolation. citeturn36search1turn36search7turn23search2turn29search1turn29search14

Developmental integration is especially plausible because the nervous system can learn to interpret new channels. Cochlear-implant research strongly supports sensitive periods and early plasticity, and sensory-substitution work shows that the brain can extract useful percepts from signals delivered through non-native channels when training and active exploration are present. So a child raised with a co-resident silicon interface from infancy is a much more plausible fictional construct than an adult suddenly receiving a full parallel substrate. citeturn23search9turn23search13turn29search1turn29search7turn29search17

## Open questions and limitations

Several elements remain genuinely open. No one has demonstrated a self-sustaining silicon-based metabolism compatible with mammalian physiology. No one has restored vision through whole-eye transplantation in humans. Neural decoding still recovers correlated representational content rather than full subjective experience. Memory extraction from preserved brain structure remains theoretical and controversial. And quantum-brain proposals remain speculative, with no established evidence that they explain ordinary cognition, traumatic “imprints,” or consciousness better than classical neuroscience. Those unresolved points are not defects in your theme; they are the boundaries that separate rigorous extrapolation from unsupported assertion. citeturn6search0turn37view0turn12view3turn25view2turn20search11
