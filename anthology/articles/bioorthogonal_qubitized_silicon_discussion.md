### 1.1 ~ Carbon nanoallotropes

The best umbrella term for what you are describing is **carbon nanoallotropes** or, more simply, **nanocarbons / carbon nanostructures**. In the literature, this umbrella includes fullerenes, carbon nanotubes, graphene, nanodiamonds, carbon dots, and **combined superstructures** built by joining or nesting these motifs. That is the closest formal category to buckyballs, nanotubes, nanobuds, peapods, and graphullerene-type materials. ([PubMed][1])

If you want the narrower breakdown: **buckyballs** are fullerenes; **carbon nanotubes** are 1D nanocarbons; **carbon nanobuds** are fullerene–nanotube hybrid nanostructures; **carbon peapods** are fullerenes encapsulated inside nanotubes; and **graphullerene / graphullerite** refers to a newer fullerene-based extended carbon material, where graphullerene is the 2D sheet form and graphullerite is the bulk crystal form from which it can be exfoliated. ([Goldbook][2])

Your instinct that this goes beyond **fullerenes** is correct. In the strict IUPAC sense, a fullerene is a **closed cage** made entirely of carbon atoms in a cage-like fused-ring system. That definition fits C60 and related cages, but it is too narrow for the whole design space you mean, especially once you include graphene-like sheets, nanotubes, and hybrid architectures. ([Goldbook][2])

For the broader design idea—stable, atomically organized architectures whose useful properties emerge from the larger repeating pattern rather than isolated atoms—the best field-level term is **nanoarchitectonics** or **molecular architectonics**. For carbon specifically, people also use phrases like **nanocarbon architectonics** and **molecular nanocarbon science** for this kind of atomically designed carbon structure building. ([Nature][3])

So the most accurate single answer is: **carbon nanoallotropes**, and the broader design philosophy behind them is **nanoarchitectonics**.

### 2.2 ~ Silicon allotropes and atomically precise silicon nanostructures

Yes, but the closer term is **silicon**, not **silicone**. **Silicone** means polymeric siloxanes, not elemental crystal/network structures, so it is not the right family for what you are after. For atomically engineered electronic structures, the relevant umbrella terms are **silicon allotropes**, **low-dimensional silicon nanostructures**, and, for the 2D graphene-like subclass, **xenes** with **silicene** as the silicon member. ([Goldbook][1])

So the answer is: **yes, there is a parallel design space**, but it is not as broad or naturally stable as carbon’s. Carbon readily supports stable sp² networks such as graphene, nanotubes, and fullerenes. Silicon does have analogous forms—most notably **silicene**, **silicon nanotubes**, **cage clusters**, and **open-framework clathrate allotropes**—but many of them are more fragile, more substrate-dependent, or more metastable than the carbon versions. ([MDPI][2])

The main silicon-side classes most relevant to your idea are these. **Silicene** is the graphene-analogue: a 2D allotrope of silicon and part of the Xene family. **Silicon nanotubes** are the nanotube-analogue. **Silicon cage clusters** and metal-stabilized silicon cages are the closest analogue to fullerene-like architectures. **Silicon clathrates** such as Si24, Si46, and Si136 are open-framework allotropes with unusual electronic and optical behavior, and are one of the more serious candidates for “architected silicon” with properties distinct from ordinary diamond-cubic silicon. Reviews of metastable silicon allotropes specifically highlight direct or quasi-direct band gaps and other useful electronic behavior in these frameworks. ([ScienceDirect][3])

The limitation is that silicon does **not** give you the same easy, free-standing family of robust nanoallotropes that carbon does. In current literature, silicene is still described as strongly affected by its substrate; researchers discuss using substrates to preserve or tune its electronic properties, and multiple reviews note that silicene oxidizes in air and generally cannot simply be peeled off and handled like graphene. Some sources state plainly that pristine silicene is only grown under vacuum on a substrate. ([ACS Publications][4])

For your specific goal—**extreme precision control of electron flow and minimizing unwanted crossing paths**—the most promising silicon direction is probably **not** “silicon buckyballs” by themselves. The more relevant branch is **atomically precise silicon electronics**, especially **dangling-bond circuits** and **silicon quantum-dot arrays** built on hydrogen-terminated silicon surfaces. Recent reviews describe single dangling bonds on silicon as atomically placed quantum dots and discuss logic architectures based on Coulombic interactions rather than conventional current flow. That is much closer to the idea of deliberately constrained, non-overlapping pathways and engineered electron behavior at the single-atom scale. ([PMC][5])

So the most accurate single formulation is this: the silicon-side analogue is the broader class of **low-dimensional silicon allotropes / silicon nanostructures**, with important members including **silicene, silicon nanotubes, silicon cage clusters, and silicon clathrates**. But if your end goal is **precise routing of electronic behavior**, the more practical and advanced silicon class to study is **atomically precise silicon dangling-bond and quantum-dot architectures**, not just fullerene-like silicon shapes. ([MDPI][2])

### 3.1 ~ The workable class is bioorthogonal silicon-based biomaterials, not a second silicon biology

For the application you described, the most accurate framing is **bioorthogonal silicon-based biomaterials and bioelectronic architectures**. It is **not** a true parallel to carbon nanoallotropes in the sense of “silicon life” or a second protein-like biosphere inside a mammal. Current chemistry supports the first idea far more than the second. Bioorthogonal systems are defined by operating in living tissue without materially interfering with native biochemistry, and that is much closer to your goal of a coexisting, insulated, selectively interoperable subsystem. ([OUP Academic][1])

The first correction is terminology. **Silicone** usually means **polysiloxane polymers** such as PDMS, which are widely used because they are flexible, chemically stable, biodurable, and relatively biocompatible. **Silicon** means the elemental semiconductor and its allotropes or related inorganic materials. For a body-coexisting extension layer, both matter, but they play different roles: **silicones/polysiloxanes** are good for soft, inert scaffolds, encapsulation, insulation, and microfluidic structures, while **silicon, porous silicon, and silicon carbide** are more relevant for sensing, semiconducting, and electrical interfaces. ([PMC][2])

If you want a “building block” family on the silicon side, the closest classes are **organosilicon biomaterials**, **polysiloxanes**, **polyhedral oligomeric silsesquioxanes (POSS)**, **porous silicon**, **silicon carbide**, and, more speculatively for electronic function, **polysilanes**. POSS is especially close to your “repeatable competent for further unique designs” idea because it is a nanoscale cage-like silsesquioxane motif that can be incorporated into larger hybrid materials with tunable mechanical and biomedical properties. ([PubMed][3])

The part of your idea that is realistic today is **a dual-material physiological stack**, not **a dual biology**. In practice that means a carbon-based organism remains the living system, while a silicon/silicone subsystem serves as an **orthogonal scaffold, conduit, sensor, actuator, or computational layer**. That is already close to how many implantable and bioelectronic systems are conceived: the biological side handles metabolism, repair, and signaling; the synthetic side handles insulation, routing, sensing, stimulation, and controlled interfacing. ([Springer][4])

The part that is **not** realistic, at least with present science, is the idea of a mammal carrying a second silicon-based analogue of proteins, neurons, vasculature, and cellular machinery built from silicon chemistry in the same way carbon biochemistry is built from carbon. The basic chemical reason is that silicon does not match carbon’s versatility in water-and-oxygen-rich biology; the literature consistently treats silicon as a much weaker candidate for carbon-like life under Earth-like conditions. ([PMC][5])

For the neuron analogy, the key point is this: **material choice alone does not create non-interfering electrical signals**. In living tissue, endogenous signaling is largely **ionic**, not purely electronic. Crosstalk is reduced by **geometry, shielding, encapsulation, impedance control, local transduction, channel separation, and interface design**. That means the best route is not “silicon neurons that naturally never interfere,” but rather **orthogonal bioelectronic channels** that convert between ionic biological signals and electronic device-domain signals at controlled interface points. Modern bioelectronic materials research is explicitly focused on that problem. ([PMC][6])

So, for your specific vision, the most relevant material stack is this. **PDMS or related silicones** for inert soft structure and insulation. **POSS / organosilicon hybrids** for modular nanoscale building blocks and tunable interfaces. **Silicon carbide** for chemically inert, durable neural or electrophysiological interfaces. **Porous silicon** where degradability or controlled host interaction is useful. **Polysilanes** only as a more speculative electronic-material direction, not as a mature in-body biological analogue. ([PMC][2])

If you want a single name for the paradigm you are describing, the cleanest one is: **bioorthogonal silicon–organic hybrid physiology**. A slightly more engineering-oriented term is: **orthogonal silicon-based bioelectronic subsystem**. Those phrases fit your actual target better than “silicon life” or “silicon nanoallotropes.” ([OUP Academic][1])

The shortest accurate conclusion is this: **yes, a silicon/silicone-derived coexisting subsystem is plausible as an inert, modular, bioelectronic extension layer; no, a true second silicon biochemistry paralleling mammalian organic matter is not presently plausible.** ([Wiley Online Library][7])
