## I. Responsiveness to USDA NIFA SBIR Program Priorities

This proposal addresses USDA SBIR Topic 8.6, Rural and Community Development. It is written as a **planning proposal whose Phase I work takes the form of a bounded feasibility study**. The planning dimension concerns a proposed path for improving how local agricultural communities coordinate supply, planning, and market access. The feasibility dimension concerns whether that path is technically and economically credible enough to justify Phase II development.

The project is responsive to rural and community development priorities because it addresses a structural problem that affects the viability of small and independent farms as local economic actors. In rural and peri-rural communities, farms often remain operationally isolated even when nearby markets value freshness, local origin, and production practices associated with independently operated farms. The issue is not merely one of demand visibility. It is also one of fragmented information, inconsistent planning signals, weak interoperability across operators, and the absence of a coordination framework that allows local supply to be used as a coherent resource.

This proposal does not claim that a full production-ready system has already been validated. Instead, it seeks approval to conduct a bounded Phase I study that tests whether a realistic coordination model can reveal sourcing scenarios in which local farms improve their position while buyers remain at least non-worse-off.

## II. Identification and Significance of the Problem or Opportunity

The problem addressed by this proposal is that local agriculture remains underutilized even where its products are socially valued and geographically adjacent to demand. Small and independent farms frequently operate in conditions where planning, sourcing, and sell-through are uncertain. This uncertainty discourages reinvestment, limits reliable output, increases coordination friction, and prevents local agriculture from functioning as a stable resource for nearby buyers.

The project treats this as a **coordination failure** rather than a simple absence of consumer interest. Farms, buyers, and intermediary organizations often manage information through disconnected systems, informal communication, or manual workarounds. Under such conditions, local supply may exist but remain difficult to aggregate, compare, route, and evaluate against buyer requirements. As a result, local participation becomes harder to scale into regular procurement relationships.

The significance of the problem is economic, technical, and community-facing. Economically, instability in local sell-through conditions can contribute to volatility in farm planning and reinvestment. Technically, the lack of interoperable data structures makes decentralized coordination difficult to test and harder to operationalize. At the community level, this reduces the ability of rural and local agricultural networks to function as durable, self-reinforcing systems of production and exchange.

For the purposes of this proposal, the practical problem is defined narrowly: **some portion of local farm underutilization may result from fragmented coordination between farms, buyers, and intermediary institutions rather than from an absolute absence of viable demand**. Phase I therefore asks whether a structured coordination framework can identify a feasible transition pathway under bounded conditions.

### Target beneficiaries

The primary beneficiaries are small and independent farms whose operations are constrained by fragmented planning, uncertain sell-through, and weak coordination infrastructure. Secondary beneficiaries include local food hubs, grocers, and community-serving buyers that may benefit from greater visibility into nearby supply if that supply can be expressed and assessed more consistently. Rural communities benefit indirectly if farm viability, repeatable sourcing, and local reinvestment become more plausible under improved coordination conditions.

### Socio-economic framing

The proposal begins from the view that increasingly it is inequitable to work with and in local agriculture, even though local agriculture remains a central organizing force in rural communities. The concern is not simply that farms produce less. It is that the conditions under which they plan, invest, and reach markets are unstable. This instability constrains farm profitability and suppresses broader local value realization.

## III. Background and Rationale

Existing local and regional food systems show that there is no shortage of interest in models that improve coordination. Food hubs, marketplaces, recordkeeping systems, and proprietary farm-management tools each address part of the problem. However, current solutions often improve only one layer of the system at a time: transaction access, internal recordkeeping, compliance tracking, or isolated inventory visibility. They do not necessarily make multiple independent farms legible as a coordinated operational resource without relying on a central platform to own or govern the relationship.

The rationale for this proposal is therefore not that no tools exist, but that current tools do not sufficiently solve the problem of decentralized interoperability. Existing systems may help a farm manage itself, or help a buyer transact in a managed marketplace, but they often do not provide an open, transferable coordination layer that allows multiple participants to express supply, constraints, and operational context in a form that can be recombined without central ownership of the network.

This proposal positions Fruitful Network Development’s approach as a bounded attempt to test that missing layer. The project is not framed as a universal replacement for existing software. Nor is it framed as proof that all local agriculture can be made competitive through software alone. Instead, it is framed as a test of whether an open-source coordination framework can reduce some of the information and planning friction that currently prevents local supply from being evaluated and used more coherently.

### Need for a new approach

Fruitful Network Development is not simply proposing another inventory application or marketplace. The intended innovation is a foundational coordination framework that allows farms to express operational and supply information from the same system they use to manage their own activity, while preserving local autonomy. The innovation is not in controlling the network. It is in making self-organization more technically possible.

At a practical level, this approach is intended to support farm-side planning, supply-side visibility, and buyer-side scenario evaluation without requiring a single central authority to act as the sole point of truth. This distinction matters because many local agricultural systems become dependent on the success or limitations of one intermediary. The proposed framework instead asks whether a shared data model and bounded coordination logic can help expose unrealized value across a local network.

### End-user requirements

For this proposal, end-user requirements are defined narrowly. Farms need a way to represent current and expected output, capacity bands, harvest windows, location, and operational constraints in a structured form. Buyers or intermediary purchasers need a way to express bounded demand profiles, service requirements, and tolerance conditions. Phase I does not need to solve every user workflow. It needs to solve enough of the representational and coordination problem to support a credible feasibility test.

## IV. Relationship to Research and Phase II

Phase I is intended to answer a threshold question: **is there a realistic, bounded pathway by which local supply in the Summit County study area could be coordinated so that participating farms improve stability or expected gain while buyers remain at least non-worse-off?**

The answer to that question determines whether broader operational development is justified. If the Phase I study identifies a feasible window under realistic assumptions, Phase II would focus on operationalizing the minimum commitments and technical workflows needed to use the framework in recurring transactions. If Phase I fails to identify such a window, the project will still produce useful outcomes by clarifying which constraints remain binding and what would need to change before retesting feasibility.

### Expected Phase I outputs

Phase I is expected to produce four core outputs:

1. a structured minimum data model for participating farms, buyers, and scenario variables;
2. a prototype coordination framework sufficient to normalize, compare, and process those inputs;
3. a bounded simulation environment that tests local sourcing scenarios under defined constraints;
4. a feasibility assessment identifying whether a two-sided feasible window exists and, if so, under what conditions.

### Relationship to Phase II

Phase II would not begin from abstraction. It would begin from the Phase I outputs. If feasibility is supported, Phase II would focus on deployment-oriented tasks such as broader farm onboarding, live workflow refinement, recurring routing and aggregation logic, integration with more active user operations, and expansion of the basket or network size under validated conditions.

### Phase II trigger

Phase II is justified only if Phase I identifies at least one credible scenario in which both sides of the market remain within acceptable bounds. A positive result would not mean the full business model is proven. It would mean that advancing to implementation is rational. A negative result would not invalidate the entire concept. It would mean the study did not find a feasible transition under the conditions tested.

## V. Technical Objectives

The technical objective of Phase I is not to build a complete market platform. It is to determine whether an open-source coordination framework can support a credible feasibility test of localized agricultural sourcing under bounded conditions.

### Objective 1: Define the minimum representational layer required for coordination

Phase I will define the minimum structured elements needed to represent farm-side and buyer-side conditions in a form that can be compared and evaluated across participants. This includes operational attributes such as current output bands, capacity bands, timing windows, and coordination-relevant constraints.

**Output:** minimum schema / structured data specification  
**Success condition:** the representation is sufficient to parameterize the study without requiring universal standardization

### Objective 2: Build the minimum prototype needed to run the feasibility study

Phase I will develop a prototype coordination framework that can ingest structured inputs, normalize them, apply bounded constraints, and generate interpretable outputs. The prototype exists to enable analysis, not to serve as proof of a finished commercial product.

**Output:** working prototype sufficient for scenario testing  
**Success condition:** prototype can accept structured study inputs and produce scenario outputs without manual rewriting of the entire workflow

### Objective 3: Parameterize a bounded local sourcing simulation

Phase I will gather and organize the minimum set of farm and buyer inputs necessary to run a capacity-banded coordination model for the Summit County study area.

**Output:** populated test dataset and scenario model  
**Success condition:** enough valid inputs exist to run bounded scenario sweeps and compare outcomes against a defined baseline

### Objective 4: Determine whether a two-sided feasibility window exists

Phase I will evaluate whether any tested scenario yields an outcome in which participating farms improve their position while buyers remain at least cost-neutral or otherwise non-worse-off within the defined thresholds.

**Output:** feasibility assessment report  
**Success condition:** the analysis identifies either a credible feasible window or a clear explanation of why none exists within the tested conditions

## VI. Phase I Research Design and Work Plan

This section is the operational core of the proposal. The study is best described as a **bounded feasibility investigation using a capacity-banded coordination model rather than a full agronomic optimization model**. Its purpose is not to maximize biological yield. Its purpose is to determine whether realistic sourcing scenarios can be identified under observed production ranges and bounded assumptions.

### A. Governing feasibility claim

Phase I will test whether it is feasible to use an open-source coordination framework to identify local sourcing scenarios in Summit County, Ohio, in which participating farms improve their stability or expected gain while grocery or hub buyers remain at least non-worse-off under defined cost and service conditions.

### B. Decision rule

The project will be treated as **feasible for Phase II advancement** if the study identifies at least one credible scenario in which both producer-side and buyer-side conditions are satisfied within the defined geographic, seasonal, and handling constraints.

The project will be treated as **not feasible under current conditions** if no tested scenario satisfies both sides of that requirement, or if the conditions required to satisfy it fall outside the study’s bounded assumptions.

### C. Core research questions

1. What exact uncertainty is being tested?  
   Whether coordination rather than demand absence explains some portion of current local agricultural underutilization.

2. By what method is it being tested?  
   By building a minimum coordination framework, collecting bounded farm and buyer inputs, and running scenario-based feasibility analysis.

3. Against what baseline is it being tested?  
   Against current local fragmentation and a bounded outside-supply comparator, both defined explicitly in the final proposal.

4. What result justifies Phase II?  
   Identification of a two-sided feasible window in which farms improve their position while buyers remain non-worse-off.

### D. Minimum Phase I artifacts

Phase I will produce only the minimum artifacts required to test the governing claim:

- **Artifact 1: Structured input model**  
  A minimum data layer for farm-side, buyer-side, and scenario-side variables.

- **Artifact 2: Prototype coordination engine**  
  A prototype that can accept inputs, normalize them, apply bounded constraints, and generate interpretable outcomes.

- **Artifact 3: Feasibility simulation**  
  A capacity-banded scenario model that tests the presence or absence of feasible sourcing windows.

- **Artifact 4: Decision report**  
  A study output that identifies whether Phase II is warranted, and why.

### E. Study boundaries

The study is geographically bounded around Summit County, Ohio, with local or regional sourcing assumptions defined explicitly in the final proposal. It is focused on small and independent farm participation in local or regional supply systems. It does not attempt to prove national scalability, universal local competitiveness, or a complete deployment of the broader framework within Phase I.

### F. Fixed versus variable elements

To keep the study bounded, some elements will be treated as fixed and others as scenario variables.

**Fixed for Phase I:**
- geographic study boundary
- general buyer class in scope
- bounded comparator assumptions
- study timeframe
- minimum product basket assumptions

**Variable within the model:**
- coordination level
- farm participation level
- product availability overlap
- adjacency and route conditions
- waste or shrink assumptions
- buyer-side tolerance conditions

### G. Minimum data inputs

The study will collect only the data needed to run the feasibility test. These categories are expected to include:

**Farm-side inputs**
- current output bands
- acreage and capacity bands
- harvest windows or timing bands
- location and adjacency factors
- handling, storage, or processing constraints
- other operational limits relevant to the model

**Buyer-side inputs**
- bounded demand profile
- product basket assumptions
- frequency or timing expectations
- service or procurement requirements
- delivered-cost tolerance or other bounded threshold

**Scenario-side inputs**
- coordination assumptions
- routing or distance assumptions
- waste or shrink assumptions
- aggregation assumptions

### I. Modeling approach

The model will treat current output and capacity as **bands**, not point estimates. This is deliberate. The study is designed to evaluate transitional feasibility under uncertainty, not to overstate the precision of future production. The model will test whether realistic sourcing combinations exist under bounded assumptions involving transportation, timing, variety overlap, handling limits, and coordination level.

The purpose of the model is to reveal whether a feasible coordination window exists. It is not intended to serve as a definitive agronomic forecast or as proof that all farms in the study area can or should supply all relevant demand.

### J. Evaluation logic

The study will evaluate whether there is an intersection between:

- a **producer-side condition**, where farm stability or expected gain improves or at minimum does not worsen relative to the chosen baseline; and
- a **buyer-side condition**, where delivered cost, service, or other selected threshold remains at least non-worse-off relative to the chosen comparator.

That intersection will be treated as the **two-sided feasibility window**.

## VII. Related Research and Competition

This project sits in relation to, but is distinct from, several categories of existing approaches:

- proprietary farm-management systems
- digital marketplace or food-hub platforms
- recordkeeping and compliance tools
- centralized coordination models dependent on one intermediary
- software that improves internal operations without enabling interoperable coordination across independent actors

The project’s claim is not that these tools have no value. It is that they often do not solve the specific problem being tested here: whether multiple independent farms and bounded buyer demand can be represented and evaluated through a shared coordination layer without requiring centralized ownership of the network.

### Prior work

Fruitful Network Development’s prior work includes software and data-organization activity oriented toward small agricultural operations, including website hosting, data organization, and operational tooling. This prior work is relevant because it places the proposed study in continuity with actual user-facing development rather than pure theory.

### Distinguishing contribution

The distinguishing contribution proposed here is a schema-first, open-source coordination approach. Rather than building value only inside a closed application or marketplace, the project aims to test whether interoperability itself can become a meaningful coordination primitive for local agricultural use.

## VIII. Market Opportunity and Commercialization Path

This proposal intentionally leaves detailed market sizing and commercial argumentation for later insertion. Even so, the commercialization path should be legible in outline form.

Phase I is not intended to commercialize a final system. It is intended to determine whether the technical and economic conditions for commercialization are present. If Phase I identifies a feasible window, Phase II would move from analysis toward operational deployment. The early commercialization logic is therefore conditional:

- **If Phase I is positive:** operationalize the validated minimum commitments and workflows.
- **If Phase I is negative:** refine the model, change the study boundary, or suspend the implementation path until the limiting constraints are better understood.

### Target user classes

Primary user classes are expected to include small and independent farms that need structured planning and coordination support. Secondary user classes may include local food hubs, grocers, cooperatives, and other bounded buyer or intermediary entities.

### Business-model direction

The business-model direction remains open at the level of exact final packaging, but the project is oriented toward an open-source core with practical service, tooling, and implementation layers that do not depend on monopolizing the underlying network.

## X. Anticipated Significance

The significance of the proposed work lies in its disciplined treatment of a problem that is often discussed too broadly. Rather than claiming to solve local agriculture in general, this proposal isolates one actionable uncertainty: whether improved coordination and legibility can create a feasible transition pathway under bounded conditions.

A positive result would matter because it would show that some portion of current underutilization is not merely an unavoidable economic fact, but a condition shaped by information, planning, and coordination friction that can be tested and reduced. A negative result would also matter because it would clarify which constraints remain binding and would prevent later development from being pursued on the basis of vague optimism.

This proposal therefore treats Phase I as a decision-oriented technical study. Its value lies in making the next step rational, whether that next step is implementation, revision, or suspension of the current path.

## XI. Conclusion

This narrative proposes a bounded Phase I study intended to test whether an open-source coordination framework can identify realistic local sourcing scenarios in Summit County, Ohio, in which participating farms improve their position while buyers remain at least non-worse-off.

The proposal is structurally a planning proposal, but the work it advances is a research-style feasibility investigation. That hybrid is deliberate. The plan being proposed is not immediate deployment of a full system. It is a disciplined test of whether deployment is justified.

The proposal therefore supports one decision: whether USDA should support an eight-month Phase I effort that defines minimum data structures, develops a bounded prototype, parameterizes a local scenario model, and determines whether a two-sided feasibility window exists. By keeping the scope narrow, the method explicit, and the outputs decision-oriented, the project aligns with the purpose of a technical academic proposal and creates a clearer threshold for Phase II advancement.
