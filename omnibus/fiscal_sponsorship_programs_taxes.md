# Fiscal Sponsorship Programs vs Sponsorship Agreements

This article is a concise reference on what a **fiscal sponsorship program** is, what a **sponsorship agreement** is, and how donations should be classified and documented so bookkeeping stays clear at tax time.

This is an operational and recordkeeping overview, not legal or tax advice.

## Core definitions (minimal)

- **501(c)(3) charitable organization (the "sponsor")**: A nonprofit recognized as charitable under U.S. tax law, eligible to receive tax-deductible charitable contributions (subject to donor circumstances).
- **Project**: A mission-aligned activity that is *not* itself a standalone 501(c)(3) (or that chooses to operate under a sponsor for administrative/compliance reasons).
- **Fiscal sponsorship program**: A sponsor's ongoing program/policy framework that allows projects to operate under the sponsor's tax and compliance umbrella, with the sponsor retaining fiduciary responsibility.
- **Sponsorship agreement**: A contract that documents responsibilities, controls, and terms between parties. In this context it usually means a **fiscal sponsorship agreement** (between sponsor and project), but "sponsorship" is also used for **marketing/event sponsorships** (an exchange of promotional benefits for payment).

## Fiscal sponsorship program (501(c)(3)) vs sponsorship agreement (contract)

### Fiscal sponsorship program (the *tax/compliance reality*)
A fiscal sponsorship program exists when a **501(c)(3)** sponsor:
- accepts funds under its **own name and EIN**,
- maintains **final discretion and control** over charitable funds,
- ensures spending aligns with the sponsor's charitable purposes,
- performs required compliance, reporting, and record retention.

Operationally: **donations are made to the sponsor**, and the sponsor administers funds for project activity consistent with its policies.

### Sponsorship agreement (the *legal protection / operating rules*)
A sponsorship agreement is the document that makes the arrangement legible and enforceable. It typically:
- defines who controls funds and how decisions are made,
- defines what the project may represent publicly (name, branding, fundraising language),
- states administrative fee/retained percentage (if any),
- states disbursement method and cadence (reimbursement vs advance, required approvals),
- defines documentation required for spending (receipts, invoices, budgets),
- defines reporting (monthly/quarterly statements, year-end summaries),
- sets termination/exit terms (handling of remaining restricted funds, wind-down).

**Important distinction:**
A contract can allocate responsibilities, but it does not by itself create charitable status. The **501(c)(3) sponsor** is the entity whose compliance obligations and tax posture govern charitable gifts.

### Common confusion: "marketing sponsorship" vs "fiscal sponsorship"
- **Marketing/event sponsorship**: Payment in exchange for promotion/advertising/benefits. This is generally **not a charitable donation** (it is an exchange transaction).
- **Fiscal sponsorship**: Gifts made to a charity (the sponsor) to support charitable activity administered under the sponsor's control.

If a public page says "donate," but the arrangement functions like "pay for benefits," the transaction risks misclassification and messy accounting.

## "Charitable income" classifications: unrestricted, restricted/designated, conditional

This section is about *how to record and explain funds* so tax-season bookkeeping is clean.

### Unrestricted (general support)
- Funds are usable for the sponsor's general charitable purposes.
- No donor-imposed limits on use.
- Bookkeeping: record as unrestricted contribution revenue (or "without donor restrictions," depending on accounting terminology).

### Restricted / designated (donor intent attached)
A donation is **restricted/designated** when the donor's intent is tied to a specific use (purpose/time) *and the sponsor accepts and tracks that intent*.

Operational rule of thumb:
- If a donation flow allows a donor to select a **supported project/recipient**, treat the gift as **designated** in the records (separate ledger class or project fund), even though the gift is legally made to the sponsor.

Technical note: In nonprofit accounting, "restricted" and "conditional" are distinct concepts. The "recipient selection" pattern is usually a **donor restriction/designation**, and becomes **conditional** only if the gift includes an explicit condition (a barrier) with a right of return/release.

### Conditional (barrier + right of return/release)
A donation is **conditional** when it is only earned/recognized after a specified condition is met (for example: matching requirements, milestone delivery requirements, or other explicit barriers) and the donor retains a right of return or release if the condition is not met.

Operationally, conditional gifts require:
- clear condition language,
- tracking of whether the condition has been met,
- a process for return/release if unmet.

## Why "recipient selection" still requires careful handling

When donors can indicate a supported project or recipient, assume:
- donors believe the gift is intended for that supported party,
- the sponsor may retain a stated administrative percentage,
- both facts must be communicated clearly to avoid donor confusion and to support clean bookkeeping.

Even when the sponsor retains discretion and control, records should preserve donor intent because:
- it drives disbursement decisions and reporting,
- it reduces disputes ("I meant this for X"),
- it simplifies the accountant's year-end reconciliation.

## Required disclosure on donation pages (recommended wording)

If a sponsor retains an administrative percentage and donors can select a supported party, include a disclosure near the donation action (not buried only in terms):

**Example disclosure (adapt as needed):**
Donations are made to the fiscal sponsor (a 501(c)(3)) and support both (a) the sponsor's charitable operations and (b) the sponsored project you select. The sponsor retains an administrative fee as defined by the sponsorship agreement to cover compliance, reporting, and financial administration.

Public wording should also avoid implying that the project is itself a 501(c)(3), unless that is actually the case.

## Bookkeeping: what matters most at tax time

The bookkeeping goal is to make it trivial for an accountant to answer:
1) **Who received the donation (legal recipient)?**
2) **Was it unrestricted, restricted/designated, or conditional?**
3) **What amount was retained by the sponsor (admin fee)?**
4) **What amount was disbursed/spent for project activity, and when?**
5) **What is the remaining balance by project designation?**

### Minimum fields to capture per donation
- donor name + contact (as available/required)
- date received
- gross amount
- payment processor reference/receipt id
- classification: unrestricted vs designated/restricted vs conditional
- designation (if applicable): project/recipient selection value
- sponsor admin fee amount (or rate) and whether applied
- net amount allocated to the project fund (after admin fee, if applicable)
- acknowledgement/receipt issued (yes/no + id)

### Minimum fields to capture per disbursement/spend
- payee (project vendor/recipient)
- date paid
- amount
- purpose category (budget line)
- supporting documentation link/reference (invoice/receipt)
- approving party (if the sponsor requires approvals)
- designation/project fund charged

### Year-end outputs that make reconciliation easy
- donations summary by designation (gross, admin fee, net allocated)
- disbursement summary by designation (spend categories + totals)
- ending balances by designation (restricted/designated fund balances)
- list of conditional gifts and whether conditions were met

## Practical implications in one sentence
- A **fiscal sponsorship program** is the sponsor's charitable and compliance framework that makes donations legally and tax-wise coherent.
- A **sponsorship agreement** is the contract that formalizes controls, fees, disclosures, and reporting, especially important when donors can designate a supported party and when the sponsor retains a portion for administration.
