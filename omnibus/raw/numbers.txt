The numbering system where a sequence starts at 0 (e.g., 00–59) is called zero-based numbering or zero-indexed. The system that starts at 1 (e.g., January 1, Day 1) is called one-based numbering or one-indexed.

Here is the breakdown of why this happens:

1. Zero-Based Numbering (e.g., Lock 00–59) 
	•	What it is: The first element is assigned the index 0, often called the "zeroth" element.
	•	Why it's used: It is common in computer science, programming, and scenarios involving modular arithmetic (like a clock). In a 60-minute cycle, if you start at 0, the 60th position is 59 (60 units total: 0–59).
	•	Examples: Digital time (00:00:00), programming array indices, physical odometer wheels.
	•	Stack Exchange  +3

2. One-Based Numbering (e.g., Calendar)
	•	What it is: The first element is assigned the number 1.
	•	Why it's used: It is based on ordinal numbers (first, second, third) and is used for counting distinct items, days, or months. It corresponds more closely to everyday, non-technical counting.
	•	Examples: Days of the month (1–31), months of the year (1–12), years in Gregorian calendar (1 CE, no year 0).
