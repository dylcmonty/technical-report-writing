# Assignment 10: Report Plan

## Working Title
**Coordination Failure and Feasibility in Local Agriculture:  
An Analytical Report on Economic Incentives, Diverse Produce Supply, and Regional Data Infrastructure**

## Report Type
This report will use a **hybrid analytical approach**:

- **Causal analysis** to explain why local agriculture remains underused and increasingly inequitable to work with or in.
- **Feasibility analysis** to assess whether a coordination-first digital infrastructure creates a practical transition path for small and mid-size farms, food hubs, and regional buyers.

The report is **not** the USDA SBIR proposal itself. It is the analytical document that will support the later proposal.

## Audience
Primary audience:
- instructor
- educated decision-makers interested in agriculture, regional markets, or technical systems

Secondary audience:
- future USDA SBIR reviewers or collaborators who may read the report as background for the proposal

## Purpose
The purpose of this report is to analyze why local agriculture often fails to capture the value of geographically adjacent, diverse, and potentially more regenerative produce, and to determine whether a coordination-first platform is a feasible response to those failures.

## Central Question
Why does local agriculture remain underutilized and economically inequitable even when consumers value local, diverse, and healthier produce, and is a local-first coordination platform a feasible response?

## Secondary Questions
1. What economic and logistical conditions currently favor concentration, standardization, and monocrop-oriented production?
2. What coordination failures most strongly disadvantage local producers and local buyers?
3. Why do local agriculture’s strong points—adjacency, diversity, perishability-sensitive freshness, and relationship-based quality—often fail to translate into market strength?
4. What existing regional models partially solve these coordination failures?
5. Under what conditions could a shared data and coordination system improve outcomes for both farms and buyers?

## Working Thesis
Local agriculture is not primarily disadvantaged because it lacks value, demand, or productive potential. It is disadvantaged because the current market rewards systems that reduce coordination burden through standardization, concentrated supply, and predictable procurement. Unless coordination infrastructure is able to capture the advantages of local diversity, timing, and adjacency, local agriculture is pushed into an economic structure that increasingly favors concentration and monocrop behavior. A local-first, network-later coordination platform may provide a feasible way to reduce these burdens if it can create non-worse-off conditions for both farms and buyers.

## Main Claim Structure
The report will develop the following line of reasoning:

1. Consumers and institutions often value local, sustainable, and healthier food.
2. That value does not automatically become market advantage because the dominant system rewards procurement simplicity and logistical predictability.
3. Small and mid-size farms face coordination failures such as demand uncertainty, fragmented supply visibility, perishability risk, and overhead from disconnected tools.
4. Existing regional models show that aggregation, processing, shelf access, repeatable outlets, and subscription demand can partially solve these failures.
5. A local-first digital platform that begins with internal farm operations and extends into external coordination may create a feasible transition path.
6. That transition is bounded by real constraints such as distance adjacency, product variety, seasonality, service expectations, and handling capacity.

## Planned Report Sections

### I. Introduction
This section will introduce the contradiction at the center of the report: local agriculture is often more valued in principle, yet remains economically disadvantaged in practice. It will establish the report’s purpose, audience, and hybrid analytical method.

### II. Background: Why Local Value Does Not Automatically Become Market Advantage
This section will explain that local demand and product value are not enough by themselves. It will show that the current produce economy is highly concentrated and that standardization and coordination simplicity are rewarded.

### III. Economic Incentives That Favor Concentration and Monocrop Behavior
This section will analyze how current market structures favor procurement systems built around repeatability, standardized product flow, and minimized coordination friction. It will contrast those incentives with the value of diverse, place-based, and more regenerative production.

### IV. Coordination Failure as the Central Operational Problem
This section will present coordination failure as the report’s main explanatory concept. It will analyze fragmented supply visibility, unstable sourcing relationships, disconnected operational tools, perishability, and buyer-side friction.

### V. Why Diverse and Regenerative Production Remain Economically Disadvantaged
This section will explain that healthier, more diverse, and potentially more regenerative production does not reliably win in the current market because the incentive structure is not designed to reward that value when coordination is difficult.

### VI. Existing Regional Models That Partially Solve the Problem
This section will compare existing local and regional operating models that reduce risk and increase coordination, such as aggregation, processing, cooperative branding, shelf access, and delivery systems.

### VII. Feasibility Window for a Coordination-First Platform
This section will assess the conditions under which a coordination-first platform could improve outcomes for both farms and buyers. It will discuss feasibility in terms of:
- sell-through certainty
- reduced volatility
- route density
- cost parity or acceptable cost tolerance
- adjacency and service constraints

### VIII. Implications for Fruitful Network Development’s Platform Direction
This section will explain how the FND platform responds to the problem by beginning with internal farm operations and extending outward into external coordination without requiring centralized control over farm data or operations.

### IX. Conclusion and Recommendation
This section will answer the report’s three main questions:
- What do we know?
- What conclusions follow?
- What should be done next?

The recommendation will likely be that a bounded Phase I feasibility effort is justified.

To keep the report appropriate for the class assignment, I will avoid turning it into the full USDA proposal.

- serve as a funding request
- foreground budget, staffing, or commercialization in the way the proposal must
- rely heavily on abstract Mycite terminology

The report **will**:
- stay analytical
- use evidence and examples
- define the problem clearly
- explain feasibility in bounded and realistic terms
- create a strong foundation for the later proposal assignment

## Expected Conclusion
The report is expected to conclude that local agriculture’s disadvantage is strongly tied to coordination cost and incentive structure rather than simple absence of demand. It will likely conclude that a local-first, network-later coordination platform is a plausible and bounded feasibility direction, especially when focused on reducing fragmentation, improving visibility, and capturing the advantages of diverse local supply under realistic regional constraints.
