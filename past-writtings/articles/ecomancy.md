# Ecomancy
## A design philosophy for working *with* complex ecological systems

### Why this article starts with “feng shui”
In English, **feng shui** is often described as **Chinese geomancy**—a tradition concerned with how landscape, built form, and spatial orientation relate to harmony and “flow.”[^fengshui_wiki][^fengshui_uw][^fengshui_britannica]  
That translation can be confusing because the Chinese words **fēng-shuǐ (風水)** literally mean **“wind–water.”**[^fengshui_wiki][^fengshui_ng] The “wind–water” phrase points to *forces and flows* (air, water, terrain, orientation) rather than the Western divination method typically meant by “geomancy.” A more precise way to say it is:

- **Literal meaning:** wind–water (風水)  
- **Common English description:** Chinese geomancy (a land-and-siting tradition)

### What “geomancy” means historically
In a classical Western usage, **geomancy** refers to a method of **divination by earth**—casting or interpreting marks/dots/figures in soil or sand.[^geomancy_britannica]

---

## 1) Coining a new term: *Ecomancy* (Dylan Montgomery)

**Ecomancy** is a coined term (Dylan Montgomery) for a *new school of thought*:

> **Ecomancy**: interfacing with an impossibly complex system (nature) by using **nature itself**—its patterns, feedback loops, and self-organizing dynamics—rather than relying on single-outcome, mechanistic tools that tend to disrupt the system you are trying to change.

### 1.1 The core claim
Nature is a complex, adaptive system. If your intervention treats it as a simple machine (“optimize one output, ignore the rest”), you often get **second-order damage**: erosion, pest explosions, nutrient leakage, water loss, fragility under drought/flood, or long-run yield instability.

Ecomancy proposes a constraint on method:

- Prefer interventions that **synchronize with the environment’s own dynamics** (infiltration patterns, successional growth, nutrient cycling, predator–prey balance, disturbance–recovery rhythms).
- Avoid interventions that demand the ecosystem behave like a controlled factory process.

**Analogy:** You can’t reliably “fight” a wave; you can move with it and use its energy.

### 1.2 What Ecomancy is *not*
Ecomancy is not a claim of supernatural prediction. It’s a **systems-design stance**: use *ecological computation* (the system doing the work) rather than *external coercion* (the manager doing the work).

---

## 2) Ecomancy as an engineering constraint: “intervention that belongs to the system”
An “ecomantic” method tends to have the following properties:

1) **Multi-benefit** (one action supports several functions)  
2) **Feedback-aware** (designed around observation and adjustment)  
3) **Low external dependency** (less need for continual imported inputs)  
4) **Recoverability** (does not collapse under a missed step or a bad year)  
5) **Temporal fit** (uses seasonal and successional timing, not just calendar time)

---

## 3) Methods that fall within the Ecomancy school of thought

### 3.1 Indigenous agroforestry / food forests (forest gardening; silviculture analogs)
**Food forests** (also called forest gardens) are multi-story systems that mimic forest structure while producing food and materials. The core design idea is **layering**:

- **Overstory / canopy**
- **Understory**
- **Shrub layer**
- **Herbaceous + ground layer**  
(Other common descriptions include vines and root crops as additional layers.)[^chelsea_green_layers][^usda_forest_farming]

Once established, these systems can reduce “labor” (in the modern sense) by shifting work from constant replanting and soil rebuilding toward:
- perennial growth,
- self-mulching / litter cycling,
- and functional guilds (plants supporting plants).

**Companion functions** often include nitrogen-fixing species (a general biological mechanism where plants host microbes that convert atmospheric nitrogen into plant-available forms).[^nitrogen_fixer]

#### Fire as a tool (cultural burning; pruning; pest reduction; understory management)
A key ecomantic insight is that some landscapes evolved with **frequent low-intensity fire**. Indigenous fire stewardship used burning to shape habitat, improve food resources, and reduce catastrophic fuel loads.[^nps_indigenous_fire][^usfs_indigenous_fire]

In oak systems specifically, there is evidence and historical documentation that Indigenous groups used burning under oaks to reduce pests and other destructive agents affecting acorn crops.[^tek_oaks_pdf] Research on prescribed fire in oak systems also examines reductions in insect infestation under certain conditions.[^halpern_2022]

**Ecomancy framing:** fire is not “damage,” but a *patterned disturbance* aligned with a system’s disturbance–recovery rhythm—when applied with context and skill.

---

### 3.2 Keyline design (water + landform synchronization)
**Keyline design** is a land-planning approach that uses topography to maximize beneficial use of rainfall and water movement. It identifies a “keyline” related to a valley’s keypoint and uses cultivation patterns and earthworks to spread water, slow runoff, and increase infiltration.[^keyline_wiki][^keyline_agwater][^yeomans_water_for_every_farm]

**Ecomancy framing:** instead of forcing water to behave against landform, you use landform geometry to **distribute** water across a landscape so soil can absorb it.

---

### 3.3 Hügelkultur (decomposition as infrastructure)
**Hügelkultur** (“mound culture”) builds raised beds from **decaying wood** and compostable biomass. Advocates claim benefits including improved water retention and soil warming as woody material decomposes and holds moisture.[^hugelkultur_wiki][^almanac_hugelkultur]

**Ecomancy framing:** decomposition is treated as a *designed infrastructure*—a slow-release sponge and fertility engine that the ecosystem runs for you.

---

### 3.4 Straw checkerboard (dune stabilization via microstructure)
The **straw checkerboard** technique stabilizes sand dunes using partially buried straw in a grid. It reduces wind velocity near the surface, traps sand, and supports soil formation through increased organic matter.[^straw_checkerboard_wiki][^straw_checkerboard_pmc][^qiu_2004]

**Ecomancy framing:** you don’t “fight the desert” with high-energy machinery; you introduce a simple physical pattern that changes boundary-layer flow and moisture capture so vegetation can re-establish.

## 4) How to write about Ecomancy without turning it into mysticism

A technically defensible way to present Ecomancy is:

- **Claim:** ecosystems are complex adaptive systems  
- **Design stance:** interventions should leverage endogenous processes  
- **Evidence form:** mechanism + measurable indicators + context constraints

### 4.1 Suggested indicators (pick per system)
- infiltration rate / runoff reduction  
- ground cover persistence and litter  
- soil organic matter trends (time-lagged)  
- biodiversity proxies (plant functional diversity, pollinators, predators)  
- yield stability and input dependency  
- labor/maintenance hours after establishment

---

## 5) Minimal taxonomy (so “Ecomancy” stays operational)
You can classify an “ecomantic” method as one (or more) of:

1) **Hydrology-synchronizing** (e.g., keyline)  
2) **Succession-synchronizing** (e.g., food forests)  
3) **Decomposition-synchronizing** (e.g., hügelkultur)  
4) **Disturbance-synchronizing** (e.g., cultural burning)  
5) **Microclimate-structuring** (e.g., straw checkerboards)

This keeps the term grounded: it becomes a lens for organizing ecological techniques by the *pattern they synchronize with*.
