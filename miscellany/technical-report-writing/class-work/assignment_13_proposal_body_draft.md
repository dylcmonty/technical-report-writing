# Phase I Feasibility Proposal Body Draft

## Working title

**A Bounded Feasibility Study of an Open-Source Coordination Framework for Local Agricultural Supply in Summit County, Ohio**

---

## Note on scope

This draft focuses only on the **body of the proposal**. It leaves out the appendix, budget, biographical sketches, and deep architecture formalism. It also leaves placeholders where figures or tables should later be inserted.

## I. Introduction

Local agriculture is often socially valued, geographically adjacent to demand, and associated with sustainable or lower-chemical production practices. Even so, small and independent farms remain economically disadvantaged relative to centralized supply systems. This proposal begins from the premise that the disadvantage is not explained only by weak consumer interest. It is also shaped by logistical, informational, and coordination barriers that make local supply harder to organize, route, price, and procure consistently.

The central purpose of this proposal is to define a bounded Phase I feasibility study. The study will examine whether an open-source coordination and data framework can help reveal realistic sourcing conditions under which participating farms improve their position while grocery or hub buyers do not become worse off. The proposal is therefore not written as a claim that the full system has already been validated. Instead, it is written as an academic and technical plan for testing whether coordination failure explains some portion of current local agricultural underutilization in Summit County, Ohio.

The proposal treats the problem as a feasibility question with secondary causal elements. It asks whether better coordination, greater interoperability, and simulation-based planning can identify a workable transition toward reduced overhead, increased utilization, and improved stability for both farms and buyers. This bounded framing is important because it distinguishes the study from a claim that local agriculture can universally replace centralized supply chains. The immediate goal is narrower: to determine whether unrealized local value can be made visible and usable under realistic conditions.

## II. Problem Context

The economic problem addressed by this proposal is not simply that small farms produce less. It is that local agricultural production enters markets that reward low-friction procurement, standardized delivery, administrative consistency, and reliable fulfillment. Existing research shows that local food sales are meaningful but still limited relative to total agricultural activity, and that growth increasingly depends on intermediary and institutional channels rather than direct-to-consumer sales alone. That matters because the channels capable of absorbing greater volume also impose stricter expectations for consistency, traceability, scheduling, and price discipline.

The resulting disadvantage is structural. Farms often plan production around historical sales and uncertain future demand. Under those conditions, they may underproduce, overproduce, or avoid making investments that would only become rational under more reliable sell-through conditions. This creates a cycle in which local agriculture remains partially utilized, which suppresses reinvestment, which in turn limits future reliability. The proposal therefore frames the central issue as a coordination problem rather than a simple demand problem.

This interpretation is reinforced by the broader food cost structure identified in the collected evidence base. Success in food markets depends not only on production itself, but on post-farm coordination costs such as transportation, labor, processing, retail handling, compliance, and procurement administration. Food hubs and regional aggregation models partially address these barriers, but existing research also shows that such systems face persistent constraints involving seasonality, supply-demand balancing, pricing pressure, and buyer requirements. In other words, local agriculture is disadvantaged less by total lack of interest than by the difficulty of converting interest into repeatable, low-friction sourcing relationships.

For the purposes of this proposal, the practical problem is defined as follows: some portion of local farm underutilization may result from fragmented coordination between farms, buyers, and intermediary institutions rather than from an absolute absence of viable demand. The feasibility study will therefore examine whether a bounded coordination model can identify sourcing scenarios in which participating farms experience improved stability and buyers remain at least cost neutral.

## III. Background and Existing Approaches

Prior research on local food systems helps clarify both why the problem matters and why a new feasibility study is justified. Studies of local food sales and marketing channels show that direct-to-consumer models can help farms capture more value per sale, but such models are labor-intensive and limited in volume. Larger-volume channels such as grocery, institutional, and intermediary procurement offer greater scale potential, yet they require consistency in quantity, timing, documentation, and delivery. This tension means that the very channels most capable of scaling local agriculture are also the hardest for smaller farms to enter and maintain.

Research on food hubs and regional aggregation models provides an important middle ground. Food hubs reduce some transaction costs by pooling product, organizing logistics, and building repeatable buyer relationships. However, they do not eliminate the structural pressures of price competition, fixed compliance costs, or seasonality. Existing evidence suggests that aggregation is helpful, but not self-sufficient. This is analytically important because it shows that the challenge is not whether coordination matters, but under what conditions it can create a feasible window for both farms and buyers.

Compliance research adds a further dimension. Food safety rules, retailer standards, and certification burdens operate more like fixed-cost barriers for smaller producers than neutral administrative requirements. Even where consumer demand exists, the route into institutional and retail channels is often filtered through requirements that are more easily absorbed by larger suppliers. This means the relevant barrier is not only the production of food, but also the organizational cost of entering modern procurement environments.

Demand-side research further cautions against simplistic assumptions. Studies on willingness to pay for local food show variation across products, methods, and contexts, and some field evidence suggests that generic local labeling does not reliably produce higher willingness to pay. The implication is not that local food lacks value, but that consumer preference alone does not guarantee a sufficient premium to offset coordination, distribution, and compliance burdens. As a result, any credible proposal must address structural market conditions rather than rely on preference alone.

Current software and platform approaches also have limits. Existing systems often improve recordkeeping, inventory tracking, or marketplace access within a proprietary environment, but they do not necessarily solve interoperability across multiple independent actors. For this proposal, that limitation matters because the intended contribution is not merely a new farm management interface. It is a bounded test of whether an open-source coordination framework can reduce some of the friction that prevents local supply from being organized as a more reliable resource.

## IV. Technical Concept

The technical concept proposed for Phase I is deliberately narrow. The proposal does not attempt to validate a complete commercial platform, a universal architecture, or a full implementation of every theoretical feature associated with the broader framework. Instead, it defines only the minimum technical components necessary to test the feasibility question.

At the core of the concept is an open-source coordination and data framework capable of representing participating farms, bounded buyer demand profiles, and the operational constraints relevant to local sourcing scenarios. The technical purpose of the framework is to make supply and demand information more interoperable and more transparent across farms and buying entities so that the feasibility of coordinated local sourcing can be assessed under realistic assumptions.

In practical terms, the Phase I system should be able to do four things. First, it should represent farm-side information in a structured way that supports comparison across participants. Second, it should represent buyer-side demand in a bounded and scenario-based form rather than as an open-ended market claim. Third, it should support a capacity-banded coordination model that tests realistic sourcing combinations without implying full agronomic optimization. Fourth, it should generate outputs that allow feasibility to be evaluated in relation to farm gains, buyer cost neutrality, and the identified operational constraints.

This technical concept is intentionally subordinate to the research question. The proposal should make clear that the software exists to enable measurement, scenario testing, and structured analysis. It is not being presented as proof that a final operational system already exists. Deep discussions of architecture theory, semantic formalism, or universal interoperability claims should remain outside the body unless they directly support the bounded feasibility task at hand.

## V. Phase I Research Design

The Phase I study is best described as a feasibility investigation using a capacity-banded coordination model rather than a full agronomic optimization model. Its purpose is not to prove maximum biological yield. Its purpose is to determine whether, under observed production ranges and realistic planning assumptions, there are sourcing scenarios in which participating farms improve their position while buyers do not become worse off.

### A. Core feasibility question

The central feasibility question is whether an open-source coordination and data framework can identify local sourcing scenarios in Summit County, Ohio, in which participating farms experience a net positive gain or improved stability while grocery or hub buyers experience at least cost neutrality or a net positive gain.

### B. Secondary research questions

1. What logistical and financial barriers currently prevent local farms from being sourced more consistently?
2. How much of current farm underutilization is associated with uncertainty in sourcing, sell-through, and planning?
3. Can farm interview data, acreage constraints, production bands, and buyer demand profiles support a credible transitional feasibility simulation?
4. Under what conditions do reduced transportation distance, reduced waste, and improved aggregation produce a feasible window for both farms and buyers?
5. What minimum technical components are genuinely necessary in Phase I to test this feasibility question?

### C. Geographic and analytic boundaries

The study will be bounded geographically around Summit County, Ohio, with local or regional sourcing assumptions defined explicitly in the proposal. The study will focus on small and independent farm participation in local or regional supply systems. It will not attempt to prove universal adoption, total replacement of centralized supply chains, or a fully deployed commercial system within Phase I.

### D. Evidence base

The proposal body should draw on four evidence groups:

1. class-based guidance on technical writing, summaries, abstracts, and proposal organization;
2. prior analytical writing already developed on local agriculture and SBIR framing;
3. outside research on local food systems, procurement barriers, transportation, perishability, food hubs, and rural development;
4. Phase I field inputs gathered from farms and bounded buyer scenarios.

This four-part evidence base gives the proposal both academic credibility and practical realism.

### E. Data collection plan

The study will gather interview-based and operations-based farm inputs sufficient to define current output, current utilization gaps, and plausible production or availability bands. The model should also include bounded buyer-side demand profiles and the operational constraints needed to test sourcing scenarios realistically. At minimum, the proposal should identify the categories of data required from participating farms and buyers, the basis on which those inputs will be trusted, and the minimum number of participants needed to support a credible simulation.

### F. Modeling approach

The coordination model should treat observed production ranges and realistic planning assumptions as inputs. It should then test whether feasible sourcing combinations exist under bounded assumptions involving transportation distance, product movement, waste reduction, and aggregation effects. The proposal should make clear that the model is transitional and evaluative. It is not intended to produce a definitive agronomic forecast. It is intended to reveal whether a feasible coordination window exists.

### G. Evaluation logic

The evaluation logic should be explicit. A positive result would show that there are sourcing scenarios in which participating farms improve their position while buyers remain at least cost neutral. A negative result would show that the constraints remain binding even under coordinated scenarios. In either case, the output is valuable because it clarifies whether the proposed strategy is worth advancing.

## VI. Anticipated Significance

The significance of the proposed study lies in its contribution to rural and community development through a disciplined evaluation of whether coordination failure is a meaningful constraint on local agricultural use. A positive result would not prove the complete business model. It would show that there is a plausible transition pathway worth developing further in a later implementation phase. A negative result would still be valuable because it would clarify which constraints remain binding and whether the proposed coordination strategy is insufficient under realistic conditions.

This distinction matters academically and practically. Academically, it keeps the proposal honest about the difference between feasibility testing and validated deployment. Practically, it gives the project a decision-oriented outcome: proceed, refine, or stop based on evidence rather than aspiration. The study therefore contributes to rural development not by promising transformation outright, but by testing whether a more coordinated local agricultural system can function under the conditions that currently suppress its use.

If the findings support the feasibility claim, Phase II could focus on operational deployment, broader pilot participation, refinement of software components, and more direct testing of implementation at the level of recurring transactions. The value of Phase I is that it clarifies whether that later effort is justified.

---

## VII. Conclusion

This proposal is structured as a bounded academic feasibility study rather than a claim of full implementation. It addresses the problem of local agriculture remaining underutilized despite nearby demand, and it frames that problem as one of coordination, information, and market friction rather than simple consumer indifference. Its central claim is limited: if local supply and demand can be made more interoperable and more transparent across farms and buying entities, then some portion of present underutilization may be explained as a coordination problem that can be studied empirically.

The body of the proposal therefore supports a single decision: whether it is worth advancing a Phase I feasibility study that gathers bounded farm and buyer data, models realistic sourcing scenarios, and evaluates whether a feasible window exists for both farms and buyers. By keeping the scope narrow, the proposal remains aligned with both the assignment goal and the standards of a technical academic document.
