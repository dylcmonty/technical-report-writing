# USDA SBIR Phase I Proposal Narrative (Near-Final Draft)
## Topic 8.6 - Rural and Community Development

**Working Title:**  
**A Bounded Feasibility Study of an Open-Source Coordination Framework for Local Perishable Produce Supply in Summit County, Ohio**

---

## Abstract

Fruitful Network Development LLC proposes an eight-month USDA SBIR Phase I feasibility study to evaluate whether a coordination-first software framework can improve local perishable produce sourcing outcomes for small and independent farms in Summit County, Ohio. The project addresses a structural problem: consumer and institutional interest in local food does not reliably convert into durable local procurement because farm-side data, buyer requirements, and logistics constraints remain fragmented across incompatible systems and manual workflows. National evidence shows local food sales are meaningful but still a small share of total agricultural sales, and participation in intermediated channels requires stronger reliability and coordination than direct-to-consumer channels alone.

The Phase I innovation is a local-first, network-later architecture built on an open, schema-first coordination layer. Instead of assuming immediate marketplace scale, the study tests a bounded transitional question: whether observed farm capacity bands, buyer constraints, and routing assumptions can produce at least one two-sided feasible window in which producer outcomes improve while buyers remain non-worse-off on service and delivered-cost conditions. Work includes minimum schema definition, prototype coordination engine development, bounded data collection, scenario simulation, and feasibility decision reporting. Success is defined by a replicable feasibility finding and explicit boundary conditions for Phase II deployment. The expected outcome is a decision-grade technical and economic basis for whether implementation should proceed, be revised, or be suspended under current assumptions.

---

## I. Responsiveness to USDA NIFA SBIR Priorities

This proposal is responsive to USDA SBIR Topic 8.6 (Rural and Community Development) because it addresses a rural economic coordination problem that constrains the viability of small and independent agricultural producers. The project is designed as a bounded feasibility study, not a full deployment claim, and directly aligns with Phase I expectations for a focused, testable technical investigation over an approximately eight-month horizon.

The project also intersects substantively with small and mid-size farm priorities by targeting interoperability, planning reliability, and procurement coordination barriers that limit market participation even where local demand is present. USDA evidence shows direct farm sales to local food channels are significant but structurally dependent on intermediated buyers and institutions, which require consistent data and service reliability that many small farms struggle to provide without shared coordination infrastructure [1], [2].

---

## II. Identification and Significance of the Problem

### Core problem statement

Local agriculture is constrained more by structure than by consumer indifference. Local preference exists, but farms still face procurement, scheduling, handling, compliance, and pricing systems that reward low-friction, standardized supply. As a result, local demand signals frequently fail to become local producer advantage.

National evidence supports this framing. Local edible farm product sales remain a limited share of total U.S. agricultural sales, while meaningful growth depends on intermediary and institutional channels rather than direct sales alone [1], [2], [3]. At the same time, the broader food-cost stack is dominated by post-farm marketing functions (processing, transportation, logistics, merchandising), where scale and coordination efficiencies are strongest [4]. This structure creates persistent disadvantages for producers who can grow product but cannot efficiently coordinate delivery and procurement requirements.

### Why this matters

The problem is significant across three dimensions:

- **Economic significance:** fragmented coordination raises transaction and planning overhead, suppressing stable farm revenue potential and reinvestment.
- **Technical significance:** format-level compatibility does not guarantee semantic interoperability under changing requirements; integration costs escalate through adapter maintenance and schema drift.
- **Community significance:** when local supply cannot be organized reliably, nearby institutions and retailers continue to source through longer, centralized channels despite local participation interest.

### Beneficiaries

Primary beneficiaries are small and independent farms in the Summit County region. Secondary beneficiaries include local food hubs, grocers, and institutional buyers seeking more reliable local procurement options under bounded cost and service conditions. Rural communities benefit indirectly through improved local economic circulation and stronger producer continuity.

---

## III. Background and Rationale

Current software categories, including farm management tools, digital marketplaces, and compliance systems, each solve part of the problem but often do not provide a portable coordination layer that supports decentralized interoperability across independent operators. Evidence from USDA-funded and related projects indicates strong innovation activity in farm tooling and local food platforms, but the persistent challenge remains coordinated execution across heterogeneous systems and constraints [5], [6], [7].

The rationale for this project is therefore not that no tools exist. The rationale is that many tools remain siloed by product boundary, participant type, or proprietary data model. A schema-first, open coordination layer may reduce repeated translation overhead and allow participant-specific systems to remain locally useful while still becoming mutually legible for bounded procurement scenarios.

This proposal intentionally limits claims. It does not claim universal competitiveness of all local supply, nor that software alone resolves structural inequality in food systems. It asks whether a bounded coordination intervention can identify technically and economically credible transition scenarios under realistic assumptions.

---

## IV. Relationship to Research and Phase II

### Governing Phase I research question

Phase I tests one decisive feasibility claim:

**Can an open-source coordination framework identify at least one credible local sourcing scenario in Summit County where participating farms improve expected position while buyers remain non-worse-off under defined delivered-cost and service thresholds?**

This is a transitional pathway question, not a finished-state adoption claim.

### Phase I outputs

1. Minimum coordination schema for farm-side, buyer-side, and scenario variables.  
2. Prototype coordination engine sufficient for bounded scenario execution.  
3. Parameterized local feasibility simulation using capacity-banded assumptions.  
4. Decision-oriented feasibility report specifying pass/fail conditions and limiting constraints.

### Phase II relationship

A positive Phase I result justifies Phase II operationalization (onboarding expansion, recurring workflows, integration hardening, and validation under live transaction conditions). A negative result still yields value by identifying binding constraints and explicit redesign requirements before additional public investment.

## V. Technical Objectives

### Objective 1: Define minimum representational requirements

Specify the smallest viable shared schema needed to represent producer capacity bands, timing windows, handling constraints, location attributes, buyer demand profiles, and service thresholds.

**Success condition:** representation supports bounded scenario testing without forcing full participant standardization.

### Objective 2: Build a minimum prototype coordination engine

Develop a prototype that ingests structured inputs, normalizes key fields, applies bounded constraints, and produces interpretable scenario outputs.

**Success condition:** end-to-end scenario runs execute without ad hoc workflow rewrites.

### Objective 3: Parameterize a Summit County feasibility model

Collect and encode enough bounded partner input to run scenario sweeps on plausible local sourcing combinations.

**Success condition:** input quality and quantity are sufficient to test baseline versus coordinated scenarios.

### Objective 4: Evaluate two-sided feasibility window existence

Determine whether producer-side improvement and buyer-side non-worse-off conditions overlap in at least one credible scenario.

**Success condition:** either identify a feasible window or provide decision-grade evidence that none exists under tested assumptions.

## VI. Phase I Research Design and Work Plan

### A. Study design

The study uses a bounded, capacity-banded feasibility model rather than a full agronomic optimization system. The model treats uncertainty explicitly and evaluates whether coordination can improve practical outcomes under realistic constraints. Methodologically, Phase I combines (i) structured requirement capture, (ii) prototype-based systems testing, and (iii) scenario-based comparative analysis against predeclared baselines.

### B. Decision rule

Phase I is considered feasible for Phase II advancement if at least one credible scenario satisfies both:

- **Producer-side condition:** expected farm position improves (or does not worsen) relative to baseline.
- **Buyer-side condition:** delivered cost/service thresholds remain non-worse-off relative to comparator sourcing.

Primary outputs will be reported as bounded ranges rather than single-point forecasts. Where possible, scenario outcomes will include sensitivity checks for key assumptions (participation level, route constraints, and shrink rates) to show robustness of any positive feasibility result.

### C. Fixed boundaries and variable parameters

**Fixed in Phase I:**
- Summit County-centered geographic scope and adjacency assumptions
- study timeframe and seasonal framing
- bounded buyer classes and baseline comparator definitions
- minimum basket assumptions for time-sensitive produce

**Variable in scenario analysis:**
- participation intensity
- capacity overlap and timing alignment
- route and handling assumptions
- shrink/waste assumptions
- buyer tolerance thresholds

### D. Evidence-informed baseline assumptions

Phase I assumptions are grounded in established national evidence and then localized through bounded partner inputs:

- local and intermediated channel structure evidence [1], [2], [3]
- marketing-cost and coordination burden context [4]
- food hub and institutional barrier patterns [8]
- food loss and perishability context [9]
- produce transport rate bands for scenario conversion [10]

### E. Data trust and limitations protocol

Inputs will be treated as decision-grade for feasibility (not predictive certainty) when they are:

- sourced from structured partner interviews and operational records where available;
- internally consistent with model constraints; and
- documented with explicit provenance and uncertainty bounds.

The study will report limitations directly, including local sample bounds, proxy use for national comparisons, and sensitivity of outcomes to core assumptions.

### F. Analysis and validation approach

For each scenario set, the project will compute comparative outcome deltas relative to baseline for producer-side and buyer-side criteria. Results will be summarized at minimum, median, and upper-bound parameter combinations, with explicit annotation of assumptions driving each result class. Prototype validation will be performed through reproducible input-output test cases that verify schema conformance, constraint handling, and consistency of scenario results under repeated runs.

### G. Eight-month task sequence

**Task 1 (Months 1-2): Scope and schema definition**  
Finalize research question expression, minimum variables, trust protocol, and baseline comparator setup.

**Task 2 (Months 2-4): Prototype coordination engine development**  
Implement minimum ingest-normalize-evaluate pipeline with scenario output support.

**Task 3 (Months 4-6): Input collection and model parameterization**  
Collect bounded farm and buyer inputs and instantiate scenario dataset.

**Task 4 (Months 6-7): Scenario analysis**  
Run parameter sweeps, identify feasible/non-feasible regions, and isolate dominant constraints.

**Task 5 (Months 7-8): Feasibility synthesis and transition logic**  
Produce final feasibility report, boundary conditions, and Phase II go/no-go rationale.

## VII. Related Research and Competitive Context

The proposal is differentiated from two common categories:

1. **Single-application tools** that improve local internal workflows but do not provide transferable interoperability across independent actors.
2. **Centralized marketplace models** that coordinate effectively but often require platform-level control over network rules and participant representation.

Recent USDA SBIR projects demonstrate strong demand for farm software, market-linkage systems, and data-driven tooling [5], [6], [7]. This project is complementary rather than dismissive: it tests whether an open coordination substrate can function as a shared interoperability primitive that supports multiple local workflows without requiring a single network owner.

The open-source strategy follows established platform logic: openness at schema/tooling level combined with quality and trust in reference implementation, governance clarity, and operational reliability.

---

## VIII. Market Opportunity and Commercialization Path

### Market rationale

National and regional evidence indicates durable demand for produce and ongoing local-food participation, but with strong structural constraints on scaling local procurement through reliable intermediated channels [1], [2], [3]. The commercial opportunity is therefore not "local preference" alone; it is reducing coordination friction that currently prevents local supply from satisfying buyer reliability standards.

### Conditional commercialization path

Phase I is a technical-commercial gateway, not end-state commercialization.

- **If Phase I is positive:** pursue Phase II deployment-oriented development with expanded onboarding, recurring workflows, and implementation partnerships.
- **If Phase I is negative:** revise assumptions, adjust boundary conditions, or defer deployment pending additional evidence.

### Early customer classes

- small and independent produce farms
- local/regional food hubs and aggregators
- retail and institutional buyers with local procurement targets

### Revenue direction

Commercialization is anticipated to follow an open-core implementation model: open coordination layer with paid implementation, integration, operational tooling, and support services. This preserves interoperability while supporting sustainable development and maintenance.

## IX. Resources, Facilities, and Team

Fruitful Network Development LLC is a small technical organization with domain continuity in agricultural software and data organization for local producers. The Principal Investigator combines software engineering execution with sustained concept development and customer discovery in agricultural contexts, including university-connected entrepreneurial research activity.

Phase I resource requirements are modest and aligned to feasibility scope:

- secure development workspace;
- workstation and cloud infrastructure for prototype and simulation tasks;
- project management and records systems suitable for federal reporting; and
- bounded stakeholder engagement capacity for structured input collection.

The company is positioned to execute a tightly scoped Phase I investigation with disciplined deliverables and explicit decision criteria.

---

## X. Anticipated Significance

This project is significant because it treats a widely discussed local-food challenge as a testable coordination hypothesis rather than a broad normative claim. The contribution of Phase I is methodological and decision-oriented: it clarifies whether a bounded interoperability intervention can generate practical two-sided feasibility.

Positive findings would provide evidence that some local underutilization is reducible through better information coordination and scenario-based planning rather than immutable market preference constraints. Negative findings are equally valuable because they prevent scale-up based on weak assumptions and identify what must change before credible deployment.

---

## XI. Conclusion

This near-final narrative proposes a bounded, academically rigorous Phase I feasibility study for USDA SBIR Topic 8.6. The study is designed to answer one decisive transitional question under explicit assumptions, produce minimum technical artifacts, and generate a decision-grade outcome for Phase II.

By anchoring claims in documented evidence, constraining scope to a realistic local context, and defining explicit success and failure conditions, the proposal supports rational public investment in a technically credible rural coordination innovation.
