## Established technical lineages

The oldest and closest formal lineage is the semistructured-data literature. That work explicitly described semistructured data as self-describing, with schema embedded in the data and no a priori regular structure assumed; the OEM family likewise treated data as heterogeneous, incomplete, and path-addressable rather than uniformly tabular. The linked-data lineage took a different route. RDF defines its abstract model as a set of triples, and JSON-LD adds IRIs and contexts so JSON can be interpreted as linked data and reshaped for different applications. RDF Schema and OWL then provide vocabularies and formal semantics for extension across independently authored datasets. This is directly relevant to your interoperability ambition. At the same time, order is not primitive in the core graph model: ordered collections must be represented explicitly through list vocabularies and conventions layered on top of the graph. That makes linked data a close precedent for extensible semantics, but only a partial precedent for a model in which forward and backward logical ordering is intrinsic. ## Binary self-description and canonicalization

For the binary layer, the most relevant precedent is the family of self-delimiting encodings. CBOR uses heads that encode major type and length information, supports indefinite-length arrays, maps, and strings terminated by an explicit break code, and specifies a deterministic profile with shortest-form encodings and sorted map keys. The specification also notes the self-delimiting property that no well-formed CBOR item is a prefix of another. If your phrase ŌĆ£self-terminatingŌĆØ is meant in a strict parser-level sense, this is exactly the sort of literature you need beneath it. ## What existing systems still do not solve

Across the sources surveyed here, the gap is not the absence of individual ingredients. Semistructured data handles open form. RDF and JSON-LD handle globally referenceable semantics. Property/path graph research handles navigability. IPLD handles content-addressed traversal. CBOR handles self-delimiting binary structure. Avro and RDF canonicalization handle major forms of canonical equivalence. What is missing is a widely adopted stack that makes order-bearing abstraction paths, schema-agnostic structure, deterministic binary framing, reversible cross-system mappings, and decentralized semantic convention all first-class within the same formal core. That statement is a comparative inference from prior art, and it is the central gap your proposal appears to target. ## Defensible novelty position

The most defensible novelty claim is therefore synthetic rather than absolute. In the vocabulary of your drafts, the concept is strongest when presented as a datum-native semantic grammar that unifies structural address-space generation, self-describing binary framing, dual canonicality, and reversible cross-schema interoperability. Your own notes already move in that direction when they distinguish the system from a graph database, frame it as a self-describing semantic grammar, emphasize shape-addressed structure, and pair a full-state canonical form with a minimal abstraction identity.
