# FND's startup posture is food brokerage, not farmer software

Question: How should FND represent itself in startup settings such as I-Corps or Y Combinator?

Answer:
In startup settings, FND should be represented as a technology-enabled food brokerage serving grocery stores and food hubs. The company is not primarily postured as a rural-development research project, an academic technology thesis, a professional accolade, or a free software project for farmers. Those frames may be appropriate in USDA, scholarly, academic, or professional settings, but they weaken the company’s startup representation if used as the lead explanation.

The startup-facing posture is:
FND helps grocery stores and food hubs source more usable local agricultural supply by using free open-source farmer tools and web-service infrastructure to make local farm output more visible, reliable, and coordinatable.

In this frame, grocery stores and food hubs are the primary commercial customers. Farmers are the supply-side network participants and strategic beneficiaries. The open-source tools, Mycite portal, MOS data layer, and web services are not the business category being sold first. They are the infrastructure and adoption wedge that makes the brokerage model work.

This distinction matters because startup audiences evaluate the company by market clarity, customer urgency, commercial wedge, and scalability. A startup application should quickly answer: who pays, what painful transaction is improved, why now, why this team, and why the model compounds. For FND, the strongest answer is not “we build open-source tools for farmers.” The stronger answer is “we make local food sourcing operationally viable for buyers that already want local supply but cannot currently rely on it.”

## Representation by context

### Startup / I-Corps / YC posture

FND is a food brokerage and coordination company.

The company serves grocery stores and food hubs by improving the reliability, visibility, and coordination of local farm supply. Farmer tools are free or low-cost because farmer adoption is necessary for supply-side liquidity. Open-source development is not charity in this framing; it is a go-to-market and data-participation strategy. It lowers farmer adoption friction, increases trust, and builds a wider supply network for the brokerage.

Lead with:
- buyer pain: grocery stores and food hubs cannot reliably source enough local product at usable price, timing, and volume;
- product: a brokerage layer supported by farmer-side operational tools and structured supply data;
- customer: grocers, food hubs, institutions, or local buyers with recurring procurement needs;
- wedge: free farmer web services and open-source tools that create supply-side participation;
- business model: brokerage margin, procurement service, buyer contracts, logistics coordination, or transaction-based revenue;
- scalability: the same farmer-side tooling and data conventions can support more regions, more buyers, and more categories over time.

Do not lead with:
- “we are an open-source farmer software project”;
- “we provide free web hosting”;
- “we are applying for USDA SBIR”;
- “we created a universal language of information”;
- “we are mainly helping farmers digitize records.”

Those can be true supporting claims, but they are not the startup pitch.

### USDA proposal posture

FND is a rural and community development technology project testing whether coordination infrastructure can improve the feasibility of working with and in local agriculture.

In that setting, it is appropriate to emphasize rural equity, farmer income volatility, feasibility testing, technical uncertainty, open-source public benefit, and community-level impact. The USDA posture can center the research question and societal benefit because that audience evaluates technical merit, feasibility, agricultural relevance, and rural development impact.

### Academic / scholarly posture

FND is a technical and economic investigation into decentralized coordination, interoperability, local agricultural data, and supply-demand transparency.

In this setting, it is appropriate to foreground MOS, Mycite, semantic interoperability, schema flexibility, simulation design, and the theoretical distinction between centralized platforms and decentralized data fabrics. The audience is prepared to evaluate concepts before business-model immediacy.

### Professional accolade / resume posture

FND is evidence of founder initiative, technical implementation, customer discovery, business formation, software deployment, and agricultural systems work.

In this setting, it is appropriate to emphasize execution, leadership, client work, technical breadth, community engagement, and persistence.

## The core nuance

The same company can be accurately represented in different ways without being inconsistent. The difference is the evaluative frame.

For startup audiences, the decisive question is:
Can this become a commercially valuable company?

For USDA SBIR audiences, the decisive question is:
Can this technical approach be feasibly tested and produce rural/agricultural benefit?

For academic audiences, the decisive question is:
Is the technical and conceptual argument coherent?

For professional audiences, the decisive question is:
Does this demonstrate capability, initiative, and applied technical work?

FND’s startup representation should therefore compress toward the commercial system:
local food buyers have unreliable local supply; farmers have underutilized output and limited incentive to surrender data to centralized platforms; FND uses free farmer-facing software and open-source data conventions to create a trusted supply network; the company monetizes by brokering or coordinating local food transactions for buyers that already have purchasing demand.

## Clean startup formulation

Short form:
FND is a technology-enabled food brokerage that helps grocery stores and food hubs source reliable local produce by giving farmers free open-source tools that make their supply easier to coordinate.

Slightly longer form:
FND connects grocery stores and food hubs with local farms by combining food brokerage with free farmer-facing software. Farmers receive web services and operational tools without being forced to surrender their data to a closed platform. Buyers receive a clearer, more reliable picture of local supply. The business makes money from the buyer-side coordination and brokerage layer, while the open-source farmer tools build the supply network that makes the brokerage valuable.

One-line YC-style version:
FND makes local produce sourcing reliable for grocers by giving farmers free software that turns fragmented farm output into brokerable supply.

## Boundary rules

- Website hosting is a wedge, not the company category.
- Open-source tools are a supply-side adoption strategy, not the main startup explanation.
- MOS/Mycite is the technical basis for interoperability, not the first sentence of a startup pitch.
- Farmers are essential participants and beneficiaries, but grocery stores and food hubs are the clearer startup customer.
- USDA language should not dominate startup applications.
- The company should not sound like a nonprofit unless the audience is explicitly evaluating public benefit.
- The food brokerage frame should not erase the technology; it should make the technology commercially legible.
