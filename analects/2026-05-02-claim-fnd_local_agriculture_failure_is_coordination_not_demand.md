# Local agriculture failure is coordination, not only demand

The central local-agriculture failure is not simply lack of interest in local food. The recurring failure is coordination.

Consumers, grocers, food hubs, and institutions may value local supply, but that preference does not automatically become a reliable procurement channel. Buyers need predictable availability, volume, timing, price, documentation, and delivery. Individual farms often cannot provide that reliability alone, and fragmented farm output is difficult for buyers to assemble manually.

This produces a market inefficiency: geographically adjacent supply can remain underused even when demand exists nearby. Centralized distributors win because they reduce procurement friction, not because their food is always more valued.

FND’s operating thesis is that local agriculture becomes more economically usable when supply and demand are made visible, structured, and coordinatable without forcing farms into a single closed platform.
