# Message Anecdote

One electronic message that stands out to me is a LinkedIn post I made about the work I am doing through Fruitful Network Development. In that post, I was trying to explain a question that I keep returning to: if local agriculture is geographically close to the consumer, why does it still get outcompeted by larger, more centralized systems? I wrote about how local food is often more valued by consumers, yet it still faces logistical and financial barriers that make it harder to work with. What made that message important to me was that it was not just an update about my company. It was an attempt to communicate a broader systems problem in a way that other people could understand quickly. Instead of starting with software or technical language, I started with the tension in the problem itself. ([LinkedIn][1])

What I found interesting about posting that message electronically, rather than only talking about it in person, was the way the message could work on multiple levels at once. It functioned as a public reflection, a professional introduction to my company, and an invitation for other people to respond if they worked in food procurement, distribution, or farm coordination. That made me think more carefully about audience. On LinkedIn, the audience is never just one person. It includes people I know, people I do not know, and people who may only read the first few lines before deciding whether to keep going. Because of that, I had to think about tone, clarity, and what would actually make someone stop and care. The course material on email and social media reinforces that kind of decision-making by stressing that digital communication should be focused, professional, and written with an awareness that it may reach much farther than expected.  

The main thing I learned from that electronic message is that narrative matters even in technical or business-oriented communication. The post was not successful because it listed features or explained my software in detail. It was stronger because it framed a real contradiction and then pointed toward a concrete response. It also showed me that electronic messages can do more than just transfer information. They can position a person, frame a problem, test whether an idea resonates, and open the door to further conversation. In that sense, the post helped me see that digital communication is not separate from the work I am doing. It is part of the work itself, because if I cannot explain the problem and why it matters, then the technical solution will not move people.
