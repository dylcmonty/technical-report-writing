# Terry Scholarship Personal Statement

I chose The University of Akron because it gave me a practical path to study engineering, mathematics, and computing in a way that could be applied directly to real problems around me. I completed a BA in Computer Engineering in January 2026 and I am continuing a BS in Applied Mathematics. That combination reflects how I think: I am drawn to systems where theory, software, hardware, and human need have to meet in a working result.

My interest in this field did not begin only in a classroom. I have repeatedly taught myself unfamiliar technical tools when a problem required it. I taught myself KiCad to prototype hardware, worked with C++ and Speeduino in the context of real-time engine data, built physical systems through independent experimentation, and continued developing software projects outside of normal coursework. Those experiences helped me understand why I chose this major: I want the technical ability to move from an idea to a functioning system, especially where the existing tools are too expensive, fragmented, or inaccessible for the people who need them.

That same motivation led me to found Fruitful Network Development LLC, an Ohio agricultural technology and engineering consulting company. Through that work, I have helped farms and local food organizations with web infrastructure, operational systems, and longer-term software concepts for improving coordination in regional agriculture. My work has been shaped by customer discovery through the University of Akron Research Foundation I-Corps program, where I conducted more than 43 interviews with farmers, co-ops, extension programs, grocery stores, farm organizations, CSAs, and other agriculture stakeholders. Those conversations changed my understanding of technology. I became less interested in building software for its own sake and more interested in building systems that reduce friction for people doing difficult, necessary work.

My future career plans are to continue developing software and data infrastructure that helps small organizations operate with more independence and coordination. In agriculture, that means tools that can help farms manage information, communicate supply, reduce operational overhead, and participate in local markets without being forced into a single centralized platform. More broadly, I want to work at the intersection of software architecture, data systems, and applied engineering, where technical decisions have direct consequences for communities.

A scholarship would materially affect my educational experience by giving me more room to focus on finishing my degree well while continuing applied technical work with discipline. It would reduce pressure to divide attention between immediate financial demands and the longer-term work required by engineering study, software development, research, and pilot coordination. I am not seeking support as a substitute for work. I am seeking support so that my work as a student, developer, and founder can remain focused, rigorous, and useful.
