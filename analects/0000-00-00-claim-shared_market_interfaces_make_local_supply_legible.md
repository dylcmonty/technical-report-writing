# Shared market interfaces make local supply legible

## Retrieval Gist

Why did historical local food systems work without modern software platforms?

## Claim

Historically workable local food systems relied on shared market interfaces such as terminals, auctions, and recurring public markets. Those structures concentrated buyers, sellers, storage, transport, and repeated transactions in one place, which lowered search cost, enabled price discovery, and made farm output legible to downstream demand. The important lesson is not nostalgia for a past format. It is that local agriculture works better when supply and demand meet through repeatable interfaces rather than through one-off relationships alone.

## Boundaries

- This does not claim historical systems were fully equitable or frictionless.
- It does not imply that a digital interface can replace physical handling, storage, or transport by itself.
